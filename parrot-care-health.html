🦜 Parrot Care & Health Guide

Everything you need to know to give your exotic parrot a long, healthy and happy life — from diet and housing to socialization, enrichment and veterinary care.

The Foundation of Good Parrot Care

Caring for an exotic parrot is fundamentally different from caring for a dog or cat. Parrots are highly intelligent, long-lived and emotionally complex animals that have not been domesticated over thousands of years the way dogs have. In many ways, a well-cared-for parrot is a wild animal living in a domestic setting — and that has profound implications for how you must care for them.

The four pillars of good parrot care are: appropriate nutrition, a proper environment, sufficient social interaction, and regular veterinary oversight. Getting all four right is what separates parrots who thrive and live for decades from those who develop behavioural problems or succumb to preventable illness.

Quick Reference Care Cards

🥗

Diet

  • 60–70% high-quality pellets
  • 20–30% fresh vegetables
  • 5–10% fruit
  • Seeds as occasional treats only
  • Fresh water daily
  • No avocado, chocolate, caffeine, onion
🏠

Housing

  • Cage large enough to spread wings fully
  • Multiple perches of varying diameter
  • Stainless steel bars — no zinc or lead
  • Positioned at eye level
  • Away from drafts and kitchen fumes
  • Cover at night for 10–12 hours sleep
🧠

Enrichment

  • Rotate toys weekly
  • Foraging puzzles and food-finding games
  • Training sessions using positive reinforcement
  • New experiences gradually introduced
  • Music, TV or radio when alone
  • Out-of-cage time every day
🤝

Socialization

  • Minimum 2–3 hours daily interaction
  • Consistent daily routine
  • Include the bird in family activities
  • Never punish — use positive reinforcement only
  • Respect the bird's body language
  • Avoid long separations

Diet in Detail

Why Pellets Should Be the Base

Many new parrot owners make the mistake of feeding primarily seeds. While birds love seeds (they are calorie-dense and taste good), a seed-heavy diet is nutritionally incomplete and leads to fatty liver disease, obesity, and vitamin deficiencies — particularly Vitamin A, which is critical for immune function and feather quality.

High-quality formulated pellets contain a balanced range of vitamins, minerals and amino acids. Transitioning a bird to pellets can take patience — especially with older birds — but it is worth the effort. Mix pellets with familiar foods gradually over several weeks.

Fresh Foods to Include

Fresh vegetables are a critical supplement. Excellent choices include dark leafy greens (kale, chard, spinach in moderation), sweet potato (cooked), broccoli, peppers (red, yellow, orange — high in Vitamin A and C), carrots and cooked legumes. Fruit should be limited to 10% of the diet due to high sugar content — berries, papaya and mango are good choices.

⚠️ Foods That Are Toxic to Parrots

  • Avocado — contains persin, which can cause respiratory failure and death
  • Chocolate — theobromine is toxic to birds
  • Caffeine — coffee, tea, energy drinks
  • Onion and garlic — can cause hemolytic anaemia
  • Apple seeds and cherry pits — contain cyanide compounds
  • Alcohol — even small amounts can be fatal
  • High-salt foods — chips, crisps, processed meat
  • Rhubarb — oxalic acid is toxic

Housing and Environment

Cage Size

The common mistake is buying a cage that is too small. As a minimum, the bar spacing should match the species (too wide bars allow head entrapment), and the bird should be able to fully stretch both wings simultaneously without touching the sides. For large macaws, cages should be at least 150 × 120 × 180 cm. Cockatoos and African Greys need a minimum of 90 × 90 × 120 cm.

Perches

Provide perches of varying diameter and texture to exercise foot muscles and prevent pressure sores. Natural wood branches (apple, willow, oak — avoid pine and cedar) are excellent. Concrete perches for nail wear should be supplementary only.

Air Quality

Parrots have extremely sensitive respiratory systems. Never use non-stick cookware (PTFE/Teflon) near birds — the fumes can kill a bird within minutes. Avoid scented candles, aerosol sprays, air fresheners and cigarette smoke. Good ventilation is essential.

Common Health Issues to Watch For

  • Feather plucking: Usually a behavioural response to stress, boredom or nutritional deficiency. Address the root cause.
  • Respiratory issues: Wheezing, laboured breathing, nasal discharge — seek veterinary attention immediately.
  • Diarrhoea: Can indicate infection, dietary change or stress. Monitor and consult a vet if it persists beyond 24 hours.
  • Egg binding: In female birds. A serious emergency — seek veterinary care immediately if a hen strains without producing an egg.
  • Beak abnormalities: Overgrowth or deformity may indicate nutritional deficiency or viral infection (e.g., PBFD).

💡 Finding an Avian Vet

Not all veterinarians are trained in avian medicine. Exotic birds require a specialist — look for a vet who is a member of the European Association of Avian Veterinarians (EAAV) or equivalent national body. Schedule a baseline health check within the first week of owning your parrot, even if everything seems fine. Early relationships with your vet make managing health issues much easier.

Signs of a Healthy Parrot

  • Bright, clear eyes — no discharge or crustiness
  • Smooth, well-formed feathers — no missing patches
  • Active, alert posture — not fluffed up in the middle of the day
  • Eating and drinking normally
  • Regular, well-formed droppings
  • Normal vocalizations — singing, chattering, talking
  • Willingness to be handled (for hand-raised birds)
