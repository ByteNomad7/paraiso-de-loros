Parrot Care Guide for New Owners — Everything You Need to Know

You've brought home your first parrot — now what? This comprehensive guide covers diet, housing, daily routine, socialization, health signs and first-year tips for new parrot owners in Spain and Europe.

Bringing home an exotic parrot is one of the most exciting — and initially overwhelming — experiences for a new bird owner. Parrots are not like dogs or cats. They have complex needs, long lifespans, and a depth of personality that takes time to understand and appreciate. This guide covers everything you need to know for a successful first year with your new bird.

Before the Bird Arrives — Preparation Checklist

Before Day One

  • Appropriate cage: large enough to spread both wings without touching bars
  • Multiple perches of different diameters and materials
  • Two or three starter toys (rotate these later)
  • Initial food supply matching what the bird has been eating (ask the breeder)
  • Stainless steel food and water dishes (avoid zinc, lead and plastic)
  • A quiet room chosen as the bird's main space for the first weeks
  • Contact details for a local avian veterinarian
  • Bird-safe cage cover for nighttime
  • A supply of cage substrate (paper towels are simplest and most hygienic)

The First 48 Hours

The first two days are critical for setting a positive tone. Resist the temptation to handle the bird immediately, show it off to visitors, or overwhelm it with stimulation. A newly arrived parrot — even one that is very well socialized — needs time to process its completely new environment.

  • Place the cage in its permanent position in a quiet, warm room (ideally 18–24°C)
  • Offer familiar food immediately so the bird can eat at its own pace
  • Sit near the cage and talk softly — let the bird observe you from a safe distance
  • Avoid loud noises, sudden movements and too many new faces
  • Allow the bird to initiate interaction — never force contact
  • Monitor droppings, eating and drinking throughout

Tip: If your bird does not eat much in the first 24 hours, this is normal — stress suppresses appetite. If it has not eaten by 36–48 hours, contact your avian vet.

Diet: What to Feed Your Parrot

Nutrition is the single biggest factor in parrot longevity and health after genetics. Most behaviour problems and health issues in captive parrots have a nutritional component.

The Optimal Parrot Diet

  • 60–70%: High-quality formulated pellets — Harrison's, Roudybush, Zupreem and others offer species-appropriate formulas. Pellets provide complete nutrition that seeds cannot.
  • 20–30%: Fresh vegetables — dark leafy greens (kale, chard), sweet potato, broccoli, peppers (all colours), carrots, cooked legumes, cucumber
  • 5–10%: Fruit — berries, papaya, mango, apple (remove seeds). Limit due to high sugar content.
  • Occasional treats only: seeds and nuts — they are high in fat and low in vitamins. Use as training rewards.

Never feed: Avocado (toxic), chocolate, caffeine, onion, garlic, alcohol, apple seeds/cherry pits, rhubarb, high-salt foods. These can cause serious illness or death.

Transitioning to Pellets

If your bird was not raised on pellets, transitioning can take patience — especially for older birds. Gradually mix pellets with familiar seed or soft food, increasing the pellet proportion over several weeks. Never starve a bird as a training technique — this is dangerous and causes immense stress.

Housing and Environment

Cage Placement

Place the cage in a room where the family spends significant time — parrots are social and want to be part of family activity. Avoid kitchens (cooking fumes are dangerous), drafty windows, and areas with extreme temperature fluctuation. Position the cage so one side backs against a wall — this gives the bird a sense of security.

Sleep

Parrots need 10–12 hours of uninterrupted sleep per night. Cover the cage in the evening and ensure the sleep room is quiet and dark. Chronic sleep deprivation causes significant behavioural problems. If you stay up late, consider a separate, quieter sleep cage in a bedroom or spare room.

Out-of-Cage Time

Your parrot needs daily supervised time outside the cage. This is not optional. Out-of-cage time allows exercise, exploration, mental stimulation and bonding with you. Start with short periods in a bird-safe room and gradually extend as trust builds.

Enrichment and Mental Stimulation

Boredom is one of the leading causes of behavioural problems in captive parrots — including screaming, feather plucking and destructive chewing. Enrichment means providing novel, interesting things for your bird to do:

  • Foraging: Hide food inside toys, paper rolls, or foraging boxes. Make the bird work for treats.
  • Chewing: Provide safe wood blocks, natural branches (untreated apple, willow), palm fronds and seagrass mats for chewing.
  • Puzzle toys: Puzzle feeders and manipulative toys exercise problem-solving skills.
  • Training: Regular training sessions (10–15 minutes, two to three times per day) using positive reinforcement are excellent mental stimulation.
  • Rotation: Rotate toys every 7–10 days to maintain novelty. A toy the bird has seen for six months is not enrichment.

Social Interaction and Training

Your parrot sees you as a flock member. The relationship you build is the foundation of everything. Key principles:

  • Positive reinforcement only: Reward behaviours you want (treats, praise, gentle scratches). Never punish — it destroys trust.
  • Read body language: Dilated pupils, fanned tail, raised feathers on the body (not just crest) can indicate the bird is about to bite. Respect these signals.
  • Step-up training: Teaching your bird to step onto your hand on command is the foundation of all other training. Practice daily.
  • Consistency: Use the same commands and routines. Parrots thrive on predictability.
  • Patience: Trust with a new bird is built over months and years, not days. Every positive interaction is a deposit in the relationship bank.

Veterinary Care

Find an avian specialist before you need one. An annual check-up is the minimum — including a physical examination, assessment of beak, nails and feathers, and discussion of diet. Blood panels every two to three years give an important health baseline. Establish this relationship early so that when (not if) health issues arise, you have a trusted professional who knows your bird.
