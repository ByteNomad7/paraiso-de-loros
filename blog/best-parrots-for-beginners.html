Best Parrots for Beginners — Which Species Should You Choose?

Choosing your first parrot is one of the most important decisions you will make as a new bird owner. Here's an honest, experience-based guide to help you find the species that fits your life.

The world of pet parrots is vast — from tiny budgerigars to magnificent Hyacinth Macaws. For a first-time parrot owner, the sheer range of options can be overwhelming. And making the wrong choice can be genuinely problematic, both for you and for the bird. This guide helps you find the parrot that matches your lifestyle, experience level, living situation and expectations.

Key principle: The best parrot for a beginner is not necessarily the most impressive-looking bird — it is the bird whose needs you can genuinely meet. A well-matched, "simpler" bird will give you a far better experience than an impressive species you are not ready for.

What Makes a Parrot Good for Beginners?

When we talk about parrots that are "good for beginners," we look for several qualities:

  • Manageable size — easier to handle and less intimidating
  • Forgiving temperament — more tolerant of handling mistakes from inexperienced owners
  • Lower noise level — more compatible with apartment living
  • Shorter time commitment — shorter lifespan means less long-term commitment risk
  • Lower financial cost — both to purchase and to maintain
  • Good social nature — sociable and enjoyable without being excessively demanding

The Best Parrot Species for First-Time Owners

🟢 Conure (Sun Conure, Green Cheek Conure)

Size: SmallNoise: Medium-HighLifespan: 15–25 yrsBeginner Friendly: ★★★★☆

Conures are one of the most popular starting points for new parrot owners in Spain and Europe. They are small enough to be manageable, affectionate and playful, and their intelligence makes them engaging without being overwhelming. The Green Cheek Conure in particular is quieter and more apartment-friendly than the Sun Conure.

A well-socialized conure from a reputable breeder will step up readily, enjoy cuddles and quickly learn its name and basic tricks. They are curious and entertaining birds that will keep you engaged without requiring the intense time investment of a large macaw or cockatoo.

Best for: First-time owners, apartments (especially Green Cheek), people with 2–3 hours per day for the bird.

🟢 Cockatiels

Size: SmallNoise: Low-MediumLifespan: 15–25 yrsBeginner Friendly: ★★★★★

While technically a species of cockatoo, cockatiels are in a category of their own in terms of manageability. They are gentle, relatively quiet, easy to handle and affectionate without being demanding. They whistle beautifully and can learn simple tunes and phrases. Cockatiels are forgiving of beginner mistakes in handling and generally quick to trust.

If you have never owned a bird before and are unsure whether parrot ownership is for you, a cockatiel is a low-risk way to find out. They are inexpensive, widely available in Spain, and require basic care that is easy to learn.

Best for: True first-timers, families with children, people in apartments, anyone testing the waters of bird ownership.

🟡 Amazon Parrot (small to medium species)

Size: MediumNoise: HighLifespan: 40–60 yrsBeginner Friendly: ★★★☆☆

Amazon parrots occupy the middle ground. They are not recommended for absolute beginners, but someone with some bird experience or a very dedicated first-timer who does their research thoroughly can do very well with an Amazon. They are excellent talkers, full of character, and tend to enjoy being part of family life. Their long lifespan (40–60 years) is a significant commitment to factor in.

Amazons can go through "bluffing" phases (hormonal aggression) and benefit from experienced, consistent handling. A well-socialized Amazon from a reputable breeder in Spain is significantly easier to work with than a bird from an unknown source.

Best for: People with some experience handling birds, dedicated first-timers willing to research deeply, families who want an interactive bird.

🔴 African Grey Parrot

Size: MediumNoise: MediumLifespan: 40–60 yrsBeginner Friendly: ★★☆☆☆

The African Grey is the most intelligent parrot species and one of the most rewarding birds you can share your life with — but it is not a beginner bird. African Greys are emotionally sensitive, can become stressed by inconsistency, and require significant daily interaction and mental stimulation. Mishandled Greys can develop feather-destructive behaviours that are very difficult to reverse.

If the African Grey is your dream bird, our honest advice is to gain experience with a conure or Amazon first, then move to a Grey when you understand parrot psychology better. See our full African Grey for sale page for more details on what ownership entails.

Best for: Experienced parrot owners. Not recommended as a first bird.

🔴 Macaws & Large Cockatoos

Size: LargeNoise: Very HighLifespan: 40–80 yrsBeginner Friendly: ★☆☆☆☆

Large macaws (Blue & Gold, Scarlet, Hyacinth) and large cockatoos (Umbrella, Moluccan, Sulphur-Crested) are spectacular birds but they are not beginner birds under any circumstances. They are loud, they chew everything, they need enormous space and enormous amounts of your time and energy. The emotional damage that can result from placing these birds with unprepared owners — and the behavioural problems that develop — are heartbreaking to witness.

If a macaw or large cockatoo is your goal, start with a more manageable species first. Build your skills, your knowledge and your understanding of parrot behaviour. Then, when you are truly ready, a macaw or cockatoo will be everything you hoped for. Read our macaw species guide and cockatoo species guide for details.

Best for: Experienced, dedicated parrot enthusiasts only. Not for beginners.

Questions to Ask Yourself Before Buying

Before making your decision, honestly answer these questions:

  1. How many hours per day can I realistically spend with the bird?
  2. Do I live in an apartment or house? What are my noise constraints?
  3. Do I travel frequently? Who will care for the bird when I am away?
  4. Am I prepared for a 20–60 year commitment?
  5. Can I afford ongoing costs: quality food, vet care, toys, cage maintenance?
  6. Do I have allergies? (Cockatoos produce significant feather dust)
