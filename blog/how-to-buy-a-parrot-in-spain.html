How to Buy a Parrot in Spain (2026 Complete Guide)

Whether you are looking for an African Grey, a Macaw or your first conure, this guide covers everything you need to know to buy an exotic parrot legally and safely in Spain.

Spain is one of Europe's most active markets for exotic parrots. The country's warm climate, strong aviculture tradition, and large community of specialist breeders make it an excellent place to find a hand-raised, healthy companion bird. But the market also has its risks — from unscrupulous sellers to illegal wild-caught birds. This 2026 guide covers everything you need to know to buy a parrot in Spain with confidence.

Step 1: Choose the Right Species for Your Lifestyle

Before you start searching for parrots for sale in Spain, the most important question is: which species is actually right for you? Different parrots have dramatically different needs, temperaments, noise levels and lifespans.

Here is a quick overview to help you decide:

  • First-time owner, small space: Conure, Cockatiel, or small Amazon. Social, manageable size, less demanding.
  • Want a talking bird, experienced owner: African Grey. Exceptional intelligence and speech but needs intense social interaction.
  • Want spectacle and personality: Macaw. Stunning birds but require large space, experienced handling, high noise tolerance.
  • Want maximum affection: Cockatoo. Extremely bonded and loving but produces feather dust and screams loudly.

Step 2: Understand the Legal Requirements in Spain

Exotic parrots in Spain are regulated under both EU and Spanish national law. The key legislation is EU Council Regulation (EC) No 338/97, which implements CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species). Here is what you need to know:

CITES Documentation

Most parrot species available for sale in Spain require CITES documentation proving their legal origin. For captive-bred birds sold within Spain (and the EU), this typically takes the form of:

  • EU Article 10 Annex A certificate — for Appendix I species like Hyacinth Macaw and some cockatoos
  • CITES certificate or breeding declaration — for captive-bred Appendix II species like African Grey, Blue & Gold Macaw and Amazon parrots

Always request and retain these documents. Without them, you risk having the bird confiscated and facing potential legal consequences.

Microchip or Ring Identification

All legally traded parrots in Spain should have a microchip or closed ring (anilla cerrada) for individual identification. This links the bird to its documentation. Check that the identification number on the ring or microchip matches the documentation provided.

Important: In Spain, you may also need to register certain protected species with your regional environmental authority (consejería de medio ambiente). Requirements vary by autonomous community. Ask your breeder or a vet about registration requirements in your region.

Step 3: Find a Trusted Breeder in Spain

The quality of a parrot's socialization, health and documentation depends almost entirely on the breeder. Here is how to evaluate a breeder before you buy:

Signs of a Reputable Breeder

  • Happy to answer detailed questions about breeding conditions, diet and socialization
  • Provides real photos and videos of the specific bird — not stock images
  • Has all documentation ready and transparent before the sale
  • Allows (or encourages) you to visit the aviaries in person
  • Offers after-sale support and follow-up
  • Never pressures you into a fast decision
  • Has verifiable reviews, a professional web presence and social media presence

Red Flags — Avoid These Sellers

  • Cannot provide CITES documentation or breeding records
  • Price is suspiciously low (below typical market rates for the species)
  • Reluctant to answer questions or provide photos of the actual bird
  • Wants payment in cash only with no contract
  • Bird cannot be inspected in person
  • Seller has multiple unrelated species with no clear breeding program

Warning: Wild-caught parrot trafficking exists in Spain and Europe. Wild-caught birds are often stressed, disease carriers, and illegal. Never buy a bird that cannot be traced back to a documented captive breeding record. If documentation is missing, walk away.

Step 4: Health Check Before You Buy

Before committing to a purchase, there are several health indicators you should look for — whether in person or via detailed video:

  • Clear, bright eyes with no discharge
  • Smooth, full feathers — no bald patches or damage
  • Active, alert posture — not hunched or puffed at rest
  • Normal, firm droppings — not runny or discoloured
  • Willingness to interact — hand-raised birds should not be excessively fearful
  • Good weight — you should not be able to feel prominent keel bone

A reputable breeder will have a recent veterinary health certificate for the bird. We strongly recommend an independent avian vet check within the first week of ownership.

Step 5: Prepare Your Home

Before your parrot arrives, have everything ready:

  • A cage of appropriate size for the species (research minimum dimensions)
  • Multiple perches of varying widths and textures
  • Initial food supply of the same diet the bird has been eating
  • A few safe toys (wood, rope, foraging toys)
  • Contact for a local avian vet — find this before you need it
  • A quiet area for the first few days of acclimatization

Read our comprehensive parrot care guide for detailed preparation advice.

Step 6: The First Weeks at Home

The first two to four weeks are a critical period for your new parrot. Even a well-socialized bird needs time to adjust to a completely new environment. Key tips:

  • Keep the environment calm and consistent — avoid too many visitors or changes initially
  • Maintain the same diet as before and introduce new foods gradually
  • Speak softly and spend time near the cage before attempting to handle
  • Follow the same daily routine — birds are comforted by predictability
  • Schedule that first vet visit within the first week
